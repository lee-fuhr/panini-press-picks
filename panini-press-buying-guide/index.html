How to choose the right panini press

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A panini press that evenly heats, offers adjustable pressure, and is easy to clean will serve you best in 2026. Focus on plate size, temperature control, and build quality rather than flashy extras. The right press makes restaurant-quality sandwiches at home without a steep learning curve.

What should you look for in a panini press?

Start with the heating surface. The best presses have two heated plates so both sides of your sandwich cook at once. Look for a model with independent top and bottom temperature controls, that lets you toast the bread without burning the filling. Adjustable pressure is another key feature; a floating hinge or a spring-loaded top plate automatically adjusts to the thickness of your sandwich, from a thin ciabatta to a stuffed focaccia. Plate size matters more than you might think. A press with cooking surfaces around 9 by 10 inches handles most sandwiches but may crowd a large sub roll. If you regularly cook for a crowd or like oversized panini, seek out a larger model. Finally, pay attention to cleanup: non-stick coatings are common, but higher-end presses often use ceramic or stainless steel plates that last longer and release food more reliably.

How important is adjustable temperature control?

It’s not just nice to have, it’s the difference between a soggy, burnt, or perfectly crisped sandwich. Without temperature control, you’re locked into one heat level, and you can’t adjust for different breads or fillings. A press with a thermostat lets you dial in a lower heat for hearty sourdough and raised heat for thin, delicate bread. Look for models with a wide temperature range, typically from around 200°F to over 400°F, though exact numbers vary. Avoid presses that only have a single ‘on/off’ switch; they tend to overheat and produce uneven results. A light or digital display that shows the current temperature helps you know when it’s ready to go.

What size panini press do you need?

If you’re cooking for one or two, a compact press with plates roughly 8 by 8 inches is fine. It takes up less counter space and heats up faster. For a family of four or frequent entertaining, step up to a model with a cooking surface around 10 by 10 inches or larger. Some presses even offer extra-long rectangular plates that fit multiple sandwiches side by side. Consider your storage situation, too. Larger presses can be heavy and awkward to stow away. If your kitchen is tight, a smaller press that you can stand on its side might be more practical. And don’t overlook the drip tray: a generous, removable tray catches grease and condiments, making post-cooking cleanup far less annoying.

Non-stick vs. stainless steel plates: which is better?

Non-stick coatings are the standard on most affordable and mid-range presses. They’re easy to clean and require very little oil, but they can scratch and degrade over time, especially if you use metal utensils or abrasive scrubbers. Stainless steel plates, found on premium models, are nearly indestructible, heat more evenly, and develop a natural non-stick patina with use. The trade-off is that stainless steel plates are heavier and can be more expensive. A third option is ceramic-coated plates, which combine the easy release of non-stick with better scratch resistance. Whichever you choose, never use metal tools on any non-stick surface. Silicone-tipped tongs or wooden spatulas protect your investment. And if you’re willing to hand-wash, removable plates (common on stainless steel models) make deep cleaning simple.

What about floating hinges and pressure adjustment?

A floating hinge lets the top plate rest evenly on the sandwich regardless of thickness. Without it, you’ll get an uneven toast, the top plate might not even touch a thick ciabatta roll. Most affordable presses have a simple hinge that works for standard bread, but if you often make stuffed sandwiches or use artisan rolls, a floating hinge is worth the extra cost. Some presses go a step further with a manual pressure knob. This allows you to clamp down firmly for a thin, flat panini or gently for a plump sandwich where you want to keep the interior fluffy. It’s a niche feature, but for the enthusiast who wants precise control, it can elevate results. For most home cooks, a good floating hinge is enough.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a panini press as a flat grill or griddle?

Yes, many presses open flat 180 degrees to create a double-sided griddle. You can cook eggs, bacon, pancakes, or vegetables on the flat surface. Just make sure the model you choose explicitly states it opens flat; some cheaper presses stop at a 90-degree angle.

How do I clean a panini press without damaging it?

Let the press cool completely, then wipe the plates with a damp cloth. For stuck-on cheese or crumbs, use a soft sponge or a silicone scraper. Never immerse the unit in water or use abrasive cleaners. If the plates are removable, you can wash them by hand (most are not dishwasher-safe). Wipe the drip tray after each use.

Is a high-end panini press worth the extra money?

If you make panini weekly, a mid-range to premium model (often a few hundred dollars) will heat more evenly, last longer, and offer better temperature control. Budget-friendly presses (around a hundred dollars or less) work fine for occasional use but may have hot spots and flimsier hinges. Look at owner reviews for durability cues.

Can I panini-press frozen sandwiches or bread?

It’s not recommended. Frozen bread often thaws unevenly, turning the inside soggy while the outside burns. For best results, use room-temperature bread and defrost any frozen fillings first. If you’re in a rush, some presses with a lower temperature setting can handle slightly chilled bread, but the texture won’t be optimal.

What’s the difference between a panini press and a sandwich maker?

A panini press has flat or slightly ridged top and bottom plates that press the sandwich while toasting. A traditional sandwich maker (often called a toastie maker) has hinged, pocket-shaped plates that seal the edges, creating a closed, crimped pocket. Panini presses give a flatter, more open-faced result; sandwich makers make sealed, half-moon snacks.

How do I know if a panini press heats evenly?

Check user reviews and published bake tests. Manufacturers rarely specify heat distribution, but consistent owner complaints about burnt edges and raw centers are a red flag. Presses with cast-aluminum plates tend to heat more evenly than stamped steel ones. A model with independent top and bottom temperature controls also shows the manufacturer invested in heat management.

In shortA panini press is a straightforward purchase: choose one with even heat, adjustable temperature, and easy cleaning. Don’t get swayed by gimmicks you won’t use. Read real owner experiences, pick a size that fits your kitchen and your typical sandwich, and you’ll be making crisp, melty panini for years.
